Teaching Philosophy

How My Language Learning Shaped My Teaching Philosophy

I grew up in an environment where I was exposed to multiple language varieties. As a result, I tend to express myself in more diverse ways when speaking my native language. I tend to rely on idiomatic expressions and culturally embedded references, which come from my reading and life experience, when I speak my mother tongue. I also tend to discuss topics that are rarely covered in English textbooks, such as technology and culture. However, many of these expressions and ideas are based on specific knowledge that is rarely found in mainstream English textbooks. When I try to express them in English, I first need to rethink how to say them differently. After this mental "translation," what I say often feels distant from my true self.

Years of standardized English instruction gave me a solid grammatical foundation, but also confined me to a narrow range of "safe," formal topics and registers. Breaking free from this limitation has required intentional and sustained effort. I made it a habit to browse idiom dictionaries in my spare time and revisit familiar topics through online platforms and videos. My goal was not to acquire new knowledge, but to return to my “home turf” when speaking English, rather than being limited to the abstract and impersonal themes found in textbooks, which rarely reflected who I truly am.

This experience has profoundly shaped my teaching philosophy. I believe that the goal of language teaching should be to help learners reconnect with their authentic selves across different linguistic contexts. Therefore, in my teaching, I strive to expose students to a broader range of contexts, such as online communication and casual conversations, as well as a wide variety of topics, including history, nature, and technology, among many others. My aim is to introduce aspects of language that genuinely interest learners and to open a window for them to explore how to express what they truly want to say, rather than merely echoing a textbook.

Why Identities and Backgrounds Matter in Language Teaching

This is also why I place great emphasis on students' identities and backgrounds. Understanding who they are allows me to design lessons that genuinely resonate with them, moving beyond stereotypical representations of culture. Rather than offering generic depictions of cultural backgrounds, I strive to introduce themes that are deeply relevant to their lived experiences. In my classroom, I incorporate elements such as:

  • indigenous knowledge systems,
  • local festivals and celebrations,
  • community values and customs,
  • historical events that shape who we are today.

I also make a conscious effort to include cultures that may not be directly represented among my students, because I believe humanity should not be divided by presence or borders. Representing cultural diversity in the language classroom fosters empathy, mutual respect, and a sense of belonging. It also encourages students to express their cultural identities with confidence, rather than passively conforming to a singular narrative. For example, in my CLP classroom, content has ranged from Sumerian merchants to nostalgic 90s items, from Amazonian fish to blooming branches in East Asia. Some were used as reading materials or discussion prompts, while others simply added visual interest to slides.

Ultimately, my goal is to create an inclusive and engaging learning environment where all students feel seen, valued, and motivated to participate. This approach not only supports the development of critical thinking and intercultural competence, but also prepares students to communicate effectively in a globalized world.

Language Teaching and Technology

I firmly believe that technology is essential for language teaching. Its greatest significance lies in freeing teachers to focus on the creative aspects of lesson design. Moreover, integrating technology into language instruction enables students to engage with digital tools in an English-speaking environment, equipping them with technology-related discourse and facilitating the practical use of English in real-life contexts. For example, with the support of AI, teachers can efficiently create tailored instructional materials that meet students' specific needs. At the same time, when students collaborate using tools like Google Docs or present with Canva, they naturally acquire digital communication language such as editing, commenting, and presenting, enhancing both their linguistic and technological competence. After all, in the third decade of the 21st century, technology is inseparable from daily life.

In sum, I aim to create a classroom where students feel empowered to express themselves authentically and engage with language in real-world ways. Moving forward, I hope to continue refining this approach through reflection, feedback, and innovation.
